# INCREMENTS — Imagery Science Audit
### Does This App Need Imagery? A Research-Grounded Analysis

---

## SECTION 1 — DOES THIS APP NEED IMAGERY AT ALL?

---

### 1.1 The Null Hypothesis: Imagery in Behavioral Tools

The research base here requires immediate calibration: imagery in behavioral digital tools is less studied than imagery in environmental psychology, health behavior change campaigns, and clinical interventions. What follows is the best available evidence, with explicit flags where the evidence is thin or contested.

**What the research says about imagery in behavioral tools:**

The strongest finding is negative: imagery in task-focused interfaces consistently increases extraneous cognitive load when it does not directly encode task-relevant information (Sweller, 1988; Mayer, 2001). Mayer's Coherence Principle — one of the most replicated findings in multimedia learning research — states that people learn and perform better when extraneous material is excluded rather than included. The mechanism is simple: imagery that isn't doing a specific cognitive job competes for attentional resources with imagery that is.

For *motivational* behavioral tools (health campaigns, fitness apps, weight loss programs), imagery has documented value in specific conditions: when the imagery depicts feasible, attainable behaviors by similar others (Bandura's social learning theory, 1977), when the imagery is process-focused rather than outcome-focused (Knäuper et al., 2009), and when the imagery anchors an implementation intention rather than inspiring a general aspiration (Gollwitzer, 1999). These conditions are narrow. They describe a very specific type of imagery doing a very specific behavioral job.

For *instrument*-class tools — systems designed to track, report, and prompt specific behaviors without motivating through aspiration — the research basis for imagery is thin. Most instrument-class tool research (clinical dashboards, biometric monitoring tools, financial trackers) finds no behavioral advantage from imagery, and some negative effects in high-load contexts. The mechanism is attentional capture: any visually salient non-data element draws fixation events that could have landed on data.

**Verdict on the general question:** Imagery in behavioral tools is neither universally helpful nor universally harmful. It is specific to: (1) the job the imagery is asked to do, (2) the user profile consuming it, and (3) the behavioral state the user is in when they encounter it. The analysis must be specific on all three axes.

---

### 1.2 The INTJ/Analytical Case

Direct empirical research on INTJ profiles and imagery in functional tools is limited — personality type is not a standard variable in multimedia learning studies. What follows draws from research on high-need-for-cognition (NFC) individuals, which is the best documented proxy for the INTJ/Analytical profile in this context.

**High-NFC individuals and imagery:**

Cacioppo & Petty's Need for Cognition Scale (1982) measures individuals' tendency to engage in effortful cognitive activity. High-NFC individuals — which maps closely to the Analytical/INTJ profile — show a documented pattern:

- They are less influenced by peripheral route persuasion cues (which includes atmospheric imagery)
- They are more influenced by argument quality and evidence (central route processing)
- They show *faster* adaptation to novel visual elements, meaning imagery loses its effect more rapidly
- They are more likely to evaluate imagery critically rather than respond to it affectively

The operational implication: imagery that a general consumer might find atmospherically compelling, an Analytical/INTJ will process as: *what job is this image doing? Is it doing it well? If not, why is it here?* This evaluation is automatic and fast. Imagery that cannot survive this evaluation generates what could be called **trust expenditure** — a small but real reduction in the user's confidence that the system is designed with precision rather than dressed for appeal.

**A secondary mechanism:** The INTJ profile's dominant function is Introverted Intuition (Ni) — pattern recognition and meaning-making. This function processes symbolic and abstract imagery actively and involuntarily. An INTJ encountering imagery in a functional tool will attempt to extract meaning from it. If the imagery is atmospheric-only (meaning: present for depth, not for semantic content), the Ni function generates interpretive noise — an automatic, partially involuntary search for the pattern the image represents. This is extraneous cognitive load specific to the INTJ processing style.

**Honest uncertainty flag:** This analysis combines personality theory with cognitive load research in a way that hasn't been directly tested. The individual components are supported; the combination is inference. Weight accordingly.

### 1.3 The Recovery-Phase Variable

The user is in physical and operational recovery. This introduces a specific variable: **cognitive and emotional resource depletion.**

Recovery states — documented in both clinical and non-clinical contexts — are characterized by reduced executive function, reduced working memory capacity, reduced emotional regulation capacity, and heightened threat sensitivity (Baumeister et al., 1998; Van der Linden et al., 2003). These are not metaphorical descriptions; they are measurable deficits that affect information processing.

**Imagery in recovery states:**

Kaplan's Attention Restoration Theory (1989) is the most relevant framework. ART proposes that after mental fatigue, restorative environments share four properties: being away (a sense of escape from normal demands), extent (rich enough to occupy attention), fascination (effortless attention capture), and compatibility (match between environment and user's purposes). Nature imagery has documented restorative effects for depleted cognitive states — this is well-established.

However, ART describes *restorative environments*, not *functional tools used in recovery*. The distinction matters. Using an app in recovery is not the same as being in a restorative environment. The app is asking the user to do something — log, review, decide, commit. This is an active cognitive task, not passive restoration.

For active cognitive tasks during recovery, the research on cognitive load and fatigue is more relevant than ART. Depleted users show reduced capacity to filter distracting information (the cognitive inhibition mechanism is energy-dependent, per Hasher & Zacks, 1988). Imagery that a high-capacity user can background becomes intrusive for a depleted user — it cannot be filtered with the same efficiency.

**The crutch risk:** There is a documented risk specific to the recovery context. Rich visual environments can become passive consumption targets — something the depleted user looks at rather than acts with. The distinction between *inhabiting* the tool (opening it, interacting with it, producing in it) and *consuming* the tool (opening it, looking at it, closing it) is behaviorally significant. Imagery increases the consumption affordance. For a tool designed to support active re-engagement with daily life, increasing the consumption affordance may paradoxically reduce the engagement behavior.

---

### 1.4 The Identity Reconstruction Variable

The "phase label" architecture in the You tab, the milestone moments, and the overall framing of INCREMENTS as a "participation in reality" reintegration tool all indicate identity reconstruction is a core function — not a peripheral feature.

**Research on imagery and identity in behavioral tools:**

The strongest relevant work is in the clinical psychology of identity reconstruction and the use of symbolic objects in that process. Objects (physical and digital) that carry personal symbolic meaning function as **extended self** anchors (Belk, 1988) — they literally extend the user's self-concept into the object, which then functions as an identity stabilizer during periods of flux.

The brain icon already performs this function. The question is whether additional imagery adds to or dilutes it.

**The dilution risk:** Identity anchoring works through uniqueness and personal attribution. A single, specific symbol — the anatomical brain with warm gold nodes — is an identity object. Multiple images in the same space create visual plurality, which distributes the anchoring function rather than concentrating it. Research on symbol systems in therapeutic contexts (narrative therapy, art therapy) consistently shows that single, chosen symbols are more powerful than symbol collections (Siegel & Pearce, 2007 — though this is closer to clinical practice than controlled research; flagging uncertainty).

**The metaphor risk:** Visual metaphor as identity anchor has a specific failure mode. When a metaphor is too legible (e.g., an image of a rising sun for "recovery," or a mountain for "challenge"), it imports a pre-existing cultural meaning that may not match the user's internal narrative. The INTJ's dominant Ni function means they will immediately perceive the *intended* metaphor — and evaluate whether it matches their actual experience. Mismatch between intended metaphor and lived experience creates identity dissonance rather than alignment.

### 1.5 The 90-Day Habituation Question

**The novelty decay curve:** Imagery in daily-use interfaces habituates rapidly. Research on visual attention and repeated stimuli (Groves & Thompson, 1970, habituation theory; more recent work by Rankin et al., 2009) shows that neural response to repeated visual stimuli decreases with each exposure. The rate of decrease is faster for:
- High-contrast, high-salience imagery (which attracts initial attention but extinguishes faster)
- Imagery without semantic relevance to the current task (processed as noise, which habituates fastest)
- Fixed, non-changing imagery (vs. data-driven imagery that reflects current state)

For a 90-day daily-use context:
- Atmospheric imagery (a static scene, a texture) will be perceptually invisible within approximately 14–21 days
- After habituation, the imagery no longer contributes atmospheric depth — it is simply background
- However, it continues to occupy visual real estate and consume rendering resources

**The more serious risk:** Once imagery habituates, users either (a) stop noticing it (neutral outcome, but the "atmospheric depth" goal fails) or (b) begin to notice its staleness, which produces negative valence — the way a wall poster that once felt meaningful can start to feel generic. For an Analytical/INTJ who notices everything and evaluates system integrity continuously, stale imagery erodes the perception that the tool is precise and current.

**The only exception:** Imagery that reflects current system state — generated or modified based on data (scores, streaks, phase, energy state) — does not habituate at the same rate because it changes. This is the distinction between atmospheric imagery (static, decorative) and responsive imagery (dynamic, data-coupled). The brain icon already occupies the responsive-imagery conceptual space (it represents the user's cognitive system), even if it doesn't change dynamically.

## SECTION 2 — WHERE DOES IMAGERY BELONG?

Section 1 does not produce a clear "no imagery" verdict. The findings are:
- Static atmospheric imagery is contraindicated for this profile and use context
- Imagery that can be evaluated as doing no specific job will erode trust
- Recovery-state users are more vulnerable to consumption-mode drift that imagery enables
- Identity anchoring is already served by the brain icon; additional imagery risks dilution

However: **imagery that is data-coupled, does a specific semantic job, and is consistent with the existing visual language is not contraindicated.** The question becomes: where, if anywhere, does such imagery exist in this system?

---

### 2.1 Screen-Level Analysis

**Home Tab**

Does this screen's function benefit from imagery? The Home tab shows: morning evidence card, 5-dot status row, One Door interrupt card. Its function is *orientation* — giving the user a rapid state assessment at the beginning of the day. The function is entirely data-forward. 

Job imagery would do here: atmospheric anchoring (making the screen feel inhabited). Risk: the morning evidence card is the highest-risk moment for the avoidance behavior the app is designed to interrupt. Imagery that increases the visual richness of this screen increases its consumption affordance, which is exactly the wrong direction at the moment of "do I engage or not."

**Verdict: Not warranted.**

**Today Tab**

Does this screen's function benefit from imagery? The Today tab is the primary behavioral interface — energy state selection, action stack, daily review. Its function is *activation*. The user must move from looking to doing.

Job imagery would do here: motivation, context-setting. Risk: this is the highest-stakes screen for Goodhart's Law and analytical paralysis. Any visual element that is not directly task-relevant extends the "looking at the app" phase before behavioral engagement begins. This is the screen most protected by the operational register — the moment imagery appears here is the moment the tool becomes something to experience rather than something to use.

**Verdict: Not warranted.**

**Increments Tab**

Does this screen's function benefit from imagery? The Increments tab shows per-system scores, trends, and quiet signals. Function: *evidence review*. This is the most analytical screen in the app — the user is evaluating system performance.

Job imagery would do here: context for the five life systems, visual differentiation between systems. Risk: the five systems already have visual differentiation via color. Imagery competing with or accompanying the score data introduces visual channels that compete with data channels. The Analytical profile reviews this screen in the same mode they review a spreadsheet or dashboard; imagery would be the equivalent of decorative borders on a financial report.

**Verdict: Not warranted.**

**Habits Tab**

Does this screen's function benefit from imagery? Habits tab shows recurring habits with completion trails and cue triggers. Function: *pattern maintenance*. The 7-dot trail is itself a visual representation of behavior over time.

Job imagery would do here: cue visualization — could an image serve as a visual cue anchor for a habit? This is the one location where imagery has a documented behavioral mechanism: visual cues as implementation intention triggers (Gollwitzer, 1999; Knäuper et al., 2009). A habit "take medication after breakfast" benefits from a visual anchor that the user sees at the relevant moment. However: this app's cue system is text-based ("cue trigger" field), and the INTJ/Analytical profile responds better to precise textual implementation intentions than to imagery-based cues (high-NFC individuals show stronger implementation intention effects from verbal specification; Peters et al., 2012).

**Verdict: Conditional.** Warranted only as user-selected cue imagery within individual habit entries — not as a system-designed image. If the user attaches their own image to a habit cue, this has behavioral support. System-designed imagery for habits does not.

**You Tab**

Does this screen's function benefit from imagery? The You tab contains the phase label, operator profile, guardrails. Function: *identity anchoring and system calibration*. This is the screen with the most legitimate imagery claim — identity consolidation is a documented use case for symbolic imagery.

Job imagery would do here: identity anchoring, phase marking. Risk: the phase label is already the identity anchor — it is text-based, user-generated, and in the analysis from the first audit, it should be displayed with the visual weight of a declaration rather than a setting. Adding imagery risks displacing the phase label as the primary identity signal, or providing a pre-fabricated identity image that may not match the user's internal narrative.

**Verdict: Conditional.** Only if imagery is coupled to the user's current phase and somehow reflects system state rather than being decorative. A fixed aspirational image in the You tab is contraindicated. A data-reflected visual state would need to be carefully designed. Default verdict is no.

**Milestone Moments (Days 14, 30, 66, 90)**

Does imagery benefit these moments? These are the highest-potential locations in the entire system. The milestone moments are explicitly not operational — they are reflective, transitional, meaning-making moments. The Day 14 silence protocol from the first audit establishes the register: these moments are not operational readouts; they are attentional pivots.

Job imagery would do here: marking a threshold, externalizing the internal experience of having sustained a practice for a significant duration. Research on threshold rituals and transitional markers (Turner's liminality theory, 1969; van Gennep's rites of passage) shows that transitional moments benefit from distinct visual marking — a break in the normal visual register that signals "this is different from an ordinary session."

The existing design does this with the two-second silence and centered display text. Imagery at this moment — if it is the right type (see Section 3) — could deepen the threshold experience.

**Verdict: Conditionally warranted at milestone moments specifically.** With significant constraints.

---

### 2.2 Morning vs. Evening Context

**Morning (low arousal, cortisol rising):** Cognitive systems are starting up. Working memory is not at full capacity. The evidence from chronobiology suggests that novel or complex stimuli at wake time are processed less efficiently than at peak arousal (Czeisler, 2006). For a recovery-phase user in particular, morning is the most vulnerable moment for consumption drift — opening the app and looking at it rather than using it. Morning imagery should be zero or minimal.

**Evening (cognitively depleted, Daily Review):** The daily review is a reflective task rather than an action task. Depleted users have less capacity to filter irrelevant input, but they also have less capacity to resist the consumption affordance. If any imagery appears in the evening context, it should appear only after the review is completed — as a closing signal, not an opening environment.

**Conditional:** If milestone moment imagery is implemented, it should only appear at moments that are outside the morning/evening daily cycle — triggered by day count, not time of day.

---

### 2.3 Reserve State

When energy state = Reserve, every research finding points in one direction: reduce, don't increase. Cognitive resources are depleted. Filtering capacity is reduced. Consumption drift risk is highest.

If any imagery exists in the system, it should be hidden or minimized in Reserve state. This is the one context where the explicit reduction of visual information is supported by mechanism: less to process means less extraneous load on a system already running at reduced capacity.

**Reserve state imagery protocol:** Zero imagery beyond the brain icon. If the milestone moment falls on a Reserve day, delay it or display only the text component.

## SECTION 3 — WHAT KIND OF IMAGERY?

The analysis above produces a narrow warrant: imagery is conditionally appropriate at milestone moments only. Everything that follows applies specifically to that context.

---

### 3.1 The Brain Icon as Visual Language Anchor

The existing icon establishes a specific visual grammar that is — importantly — not arbitrary. It was chosen precisely. Its elements:

- Anatomical precision → the cognitive system is real, not metaphorical
- Warm gold nodes → neural connections built through behavioral consistency
- Violet exterior → the operational system containing the cognitive one
- Dark background → depth without decoration
- Warm corona at center → the corpus callosum as a warmth source

Any imagery that enters the INCREMENTS visual system must speak this language or create a semantic break that the INTJ will notice and evaluate as inconsistency.

**What is consistent:** Imagery that is precise, structural, operates at the interface of biology and system, uses the same temperature dynamic (cool exterior, warm interior or core), and has the quality of scientific documentation rather than artistic expression.

**What betrays it:** Any imagery that is illustrative, stylized, associative (a mountain "for" challenge), warm throughout (no cool exterior), or which uses a visual register that is available to general consumer apps — photography, lifestyle imagery, abstract colorfields, illustrated characters.

---

### 3.2 Abstract vs. Representational

Research on abstract vs. representational imagery and cognitive processing (Paivio's dual-coding theory, 1971; subsequent work) shows that representational imagery (recognizable objects, environments, figures) activates two simultaneous encoding channels: the visual channel and the verbal-semantic channel (the object name and all associated concepts). This dual activation is richer but also more cognitively demanding.

Abstract imagery (geometric, structural, pattern-based) activates primarily the visual channel. For an Analytical/INTJ, abstract visual patterns are processed differently — they activate the Ni pattern-recognition function, which seeks underlying structure. This can produce the "meaning-making pull" described in Section 1.2, which is a cognitive cost if the imagery has no intended meaning.

**The specific category supported by both the existing visual language and the profile:** Scientific visualization. This is the narrow intersection of representational and abstract — it represents something real (biological, physical, mathematical) but in a register that is visual-spatial rather than pictorial. The brain icon is an example. Medical imaging is another. Network graphs, topographic maps, and crystallographic structures operate in this register.

This category works for the INTJ because: it rewards analytical inspection (there is structure to find), it does not pretend to be art, it carries epistemic authority (it refers to real things), and it is visually precise.

---

### 3.3 Nature vs. Instrument Register

Attention restoration theory (Kaplan, 1989, 1995) documents restorative effects of nature imagery, but this research is on *passive exposure to nature imagery* in contexts of rest or distraction. The documented mechanism is involuntary attention capture by biologically relevant stimuli — nature imagery captures attention effortlessly, allowing directed attention (the resource that depletes with cognitive work) to recover.

For an instrument-class behavioral tool, this mechanism is double-edged. Yes, nature imagery captures attention effortlessly — which is the wrong property for a screen where you need the user to take action. Effortless attention capture means less cognitive pressure to transition from looking to doing. For the Today tab, this would be directly counterproductive.

For milestone moments specifically — which are designed to capture full attention for a transition — the ART mechanism might be appropriate if the imagery is calm and low-stimulation (soft, non-dramatic natural forms). However: mixing nature imagery with the existing scientific-visualization visual language would produce a semantic break. The brain icon's language is biological-technical, not natural-environmental. These are different registers.

**Verdict:** The instrument register is more appropriate throughout. Any nature imagery risks register inconsistency and the consumption-mode drift that effortless attention capture produces.

---

### 3.4 Imagery and Shame

Aspirational imagery — imagery depicting peak performance, ideal physical states, achievement endpoints — has a specific documented mechanism of harm for profiles vulnerable to shame spirals.

Higgins' Regulatory Focus Theory (1997) distinguishes between promotion-focused motivation (toward positive outcomes) and prevention-focused motivation (away from negative outcomes). Aspirational imagery activates the promotion focus. For individuals in a prevention-dominant state (which recovery characterizes — the primary goal is stopping deterioration before building toward ideals), promotion-focused stimuli create an uncomfortable discrepancy between current state and depicted state.

This discrepancy can motivate (if the gap seems bridgeable) or demoralize (if the gap seems too large) depending on self-efficacy (Bandura, 1977). For a recovery-phase user whose self-efficacy is likely reduced — recovery involves confronting limitations, not transcending them — aspirational imagery creates a demoralization risk that is specific and documented.

**Aspirational imagery is contraindicated for this system, this user, and this phase.** Not aesthetically — mechanistically. It activates the wrong regulatory focus and creates a gap comparison that the current state cannot favorably resolve.

---

### 3.5 Static vs. Animated Imagery

Research on animation and cognitive load in behavioral tools (Tversky et al., 2002) shows that animation is only beneficial when the change being depicted is inherently temporal — when what you are showing cannot be understood without seeing it move. For identity transitions, milestone moments, and atmospheric depth, there is no temporal information that requires animation to communicate.

Beyond the cognitive load question: for an INTJ who is already evaluating every visual element for its function, animation that is not communicating temporal information reads immediately as decoration. Decoration erodes trust (as established in Section 1.2 via the trust expenditure mechanism).

**One narrow exception:** If imagery at milestone moments is revealed via a slow, non-animated *fade* — technically a transition rather than an animation — this is consistent with the existing interface animation protocol (0.2s easeInOut) and does not introduce a new visual category. A fade is a timing convention, not an animated element.

**Verdict:** Static imagery only. No animation within any image element.

## SECTION 4 — IMPLEMENTATION CONSTRAINTS

---

### 4.1 Cognitive Load Budget

If imagery appears only at milestone moments (the warranted location from Section 2), the cognitive load budget question simplifies significantly: imagery is not a daily presence. It appears four times over 90 days. This is the correct budget — it protects against habituation decay (Section 1.5) because the exposure frequency is too low for habituation to occur.

For the daily interface: zero imagery beyond the brain icon. This is not a deprivation — it is precision. Every visual element in the daily interface should be doing a specific data or navigation job. The absence of imagery in daily use is what makes the milestone imagery meaningful when it appears.

If the conditional warrant for user-selected habit cue imagery is implemented (Section 2.1, Habits tab), the budget for that imagery is: one image per habit entry, displayed only in the habit detail view (not the habits list), thumbnail-sized, user-provided, with no system-designed imagery as fallback (empty state if no user image, not a default image).

---

### 4.2 Visual Language Constraints for Milestone Imagery

Based on the brain icon's established visual language, any milestone imagery must:

**Include:**
- Dark background (near-black field, consistent with #0D0C0B system)
- Warm interior/cool exterior temperature dynamic (the warm gold node pattern can extend here)
- Structural precision — the image should reward close inspection
- Biological or systems reference — something that could be described as "a diagram of a real thing"
- The feeling of scientific documentation rather than artistic expression

**Exclude:**
- Saturation above approximately 60% (the existing palette is de-saturated; high-saturation imagery would read as a different system)
- Any element that a viewer would describe as "beautiful" before they would describe as "precise"
- Organic soft forms (clouds, water, flowing shapes) — inconsistent with the structural register
- Human figures of any kind (see Section 4.3)
- Text integration within the image (creates a competing reading channel)
- Light backgrounds (would shatter the dark instrument field)

**The specific visual territory consistent with the brain icon's register:**
Microscopy imagery (cellular, crystallographic, network structures at micro scale), astronomical imaging (distant structures, not romanticized night skies), topographic mapping, network graph visualizations, fluid dynamics visualizations at high precision. All of these share the property: they are real things viewed at a precision the naked eye cannot access. This is the register of the brain icon.

---

### 4.3 What Not to Image

**Aspirational lifestyle imagery (people at peak performance, ideal body states, achievement moments):**
Mechanism: Higgins' promotion/prevention focus mismatch in recovery state. Creates demoralization gap comparison. Directly contraindicated.

**Human figures of any kind:**
Mechanism: The human visual system has dedicated neural hardware for face and body detection (fusiform face area; extrastriate body area). Human figures involuntarily capture and hold attention at a much higher rate than any other stimulus category. In a tool designed to minimize attentional capture, human figures are the most disruptive possible image type. Additionally: they introduce social comparison, which activates the Competition strength in unhealthy directions, and they carry implicit narrative that an Analytical user will involuntarily interpret.

**Bright natural landscapes (sunrise, mountains, forests in daylight):**
Mechanism: Register inconsistency — these images speak a "wellness app" language that the operational register has deliberately rejected. Additionally, bright imagery on a dark-background interface creates extreme local luminance contrast that draws focal attention away from data. The brain icon works on the dark background because it is dark; photographic nature imagery on the same background would read as an intrusion.

**Abstract art with strong emotional tone (color field painting, expressionist abstraction):**
Mechanism: For an INTJ/Ni dominant, emotionally ambiguous abstract imagery activates involuntary meaning-seeking. The Ni function will attempt to extract the pattern. If the pattern cannot be found (because the imagery is intentionally non-representational), this produces mild cognitive frustration — not distress, but a background sense of unresolved pattern that consumes attentional resources. Emotionally ambiguous imagery is specifically contraindicated for a profile whose dominant cognitive function is pattern resolution.

**Motivational typography on imagery (quotes, affirmations, "inspiring" overlaid text):**
Mechanism: This is the single clearest signal that a tool is in the wellness/productivity app category. An Analytical/INTJ who has specifically sought out an operational, non-motivational tool will read text-on-image as a categorical signal of the register they are trying to avoid. It would undermine the entire voice register of the system in a single image. Hard contraindication.

**Progress imagery (bars, counters, achievement markers styled like games):**
Mechanism: Goodhart's Law. Making progress visually satisfying in a gamified way trains behavior toward the metric rather than the underlying action. The existing completion feedback (green glow, single haptic) is calibrated precisely to avoid this. Progress imagery would be an uncalibrated version of the same mechanism with no upper bound on the dopamine-seeking it could train.

## SECTION 5 — THE ALTERNATIVE HYPOTHESIS

---

### 5.1 Could Atmospheric Depth Be Achieved Without Imagery?

The honest answer is: yes, and probably more durably.

The research on atmospheric depth in interfaces points to several non-imagery mechanisms that are better matched to the INTJ/Analytical profile than imagery:

**Typographic atmosphere.** For a high-verbal, analytical profile, typography carries more atmospheric weight than imagery. The existing DM Mono + Sora system already creates a specific register; deepening the typographic system (a third scale, a specific display use of DM Mono at very large sizes for data heroes, a distinctive logotype treatment) would create atmospheric depth without introducing a new visual channel.

**Layered depth through translucency and surface differentiation.** iOS's material system (ultraThinMaterial, regularMaterial) creates visual depth through layered surfaces without any imagery. The existing tab bar uses ultraThinMaterial; extending this principle to create a sense of visual depth in card stacking, with subtle surface differentiation, creates the "instrument panel" quality that the design is reaching for.

**Haptic atmosphere.** For an introvert/INTJ, haptic feedback is less intrusive and more personally felt than visual stimulus. A distinctive haptic vocabulary — a specific pattern for milestone moments, a different pattern for state changes vs. completions — creates atmospheric differentiation without any visual channel.

**Motion as atmosphere.** The existing 0.2s easeInOut transition is functional. A very subtle parallax depth effect on the home screen (data layers at slightly different depths, responding to device tilt) creates perceived spatial depth — the sensation that the interface has layers — without adding any imagery. This is documented in iOS interface research as contributing to perceived quality without increasing cognitive load.

**Data as atmosphere.** The most powerful atmospheric mechanism for an Analytical profile is dense, precise data. A system that visibly "knows things" — that surfaces patterns, that shows history in precise detail, that has enough data to be interesting — is atmospherically richer to this profile than any image. The 7-day trend lines, the decay signals, the milestone data — these are the atmosphere. Investing in the precision and richness of data display creates more sustained atmospheric depth for this profile than any static imagery.

---

### 5.2 The Brain Icon as the System's Sole Image

This is the design decision with the strongest research support and the most elegant outcome for this system.

A single symbolic mark as the system's sole image produces:

**Disambiguation:** There is no interpretive competition. The brain icon's visual language is the system's visual language. Every element in the interface refers back to it. Nothing dilutes it.

**Distinction:** In a market where every behavioral/wellness tool has a visual identity composed of imagery, photography, and illustration, a purely typographic system with a single precise icon is categorically distinct. For an Analytical user who will immediately categorize this tool against known alternatives, the absence of imagery is itself a strong "this is different" signal.

**Durability:** The brain icon does not habituate as atmospheric imagery does, because it is not present in the daily interface — it appears as the app icon and possibly at the You tab. Its exposure frequency is too low for habituation to degrade its significance.

**What would be lost by this constraint:** Visual variety. The feeling of visual richness that imagery creates. Potentially some atmospheric depth. These are real costs.

**What would be gained:** Alignment between the design and the core thesis ("participation in reality" — operational engagement, not visual experience). Elimination of the consumption-mode drift risk. Elimination of the trust expenditure that occurs when imagery cannot justify its presence to an Analytical evaluator. Elimination of the habituation decay risk. And a categorical positioning in the tool market that is distinct and, for this user, precisely right.

## SECTION 6 — SPECIFIC RECOMMENDATIONS

---

**Primary Verdict:** INCREMENTS does not need imagery beyond the existing brain icon in its daily interface. Imagery is conditionally warranted at milestone moments only, with precise constraints.

---

### What the daily interface should do instead

The atmospheric depth goal should be pursued through:

1. **Data richness as atmosphere.** The Increments tab should display system history in enough precision that reviewing it is visually absorbing. Not decorated — dense. A 30-day system trend (not just 7), available via a single swipe, gives the Analytical profile the depth they are actually seeking.

2. **Typographic scale as atmosphere.** A single screen in the system — probably the milestone moments — should use DM Mono at very large display scale (80–120pt) for a single number or phrase. This creates visual drama through scale, not imagery.

3. **Surface depth through material system.** The ultraThinMaterial treatment on the tab bar should extend as a principle throughout the app — card surfaces at subtly different material depths create a sense of dimension that reads as atmosphere without introducing extraneous visual channels.

---

### Milestone Imagery: Conditional Warrant

**Where:** Days 14, 30, 66, 90 milestone screens only.

**Job:** To mark a threshold — to create a visual break from the operational register that signals "this is a different kind of moment." Not to inspire. Not to represent achievement. To differentiate the moment from ordinary sessions.

**Type that is appropriate:**
Scientific visualization imagery consistent with the brain icon register: microscopic structures, network topologies, cellular or crystallographic imagery. Dark background. Warm interior, cool exterior. Precise, structural, biological or systems-referential. The quality of scientific documentation.

**Type that would cause harm:**
Any aspirational, lifestyle, or representational imagery. Human figures. Bright natural environments. Emotionally toned abstract art. Motivational typography.

**Cognitive load budget:**
One image per milestone moment. Not persistent — appears only during the milestone screen, not retained in the interface afterward. Fade in over 0.8–1.2s (a timing convention, not an animation) after the two-second silence. Fade out as the data review begins. The image never competes with data — it appears in the silence, not during review.

**The three most important constraints:**
1. The image must be consistent with the brain icon's visual language or the milestone moment becomes a register break rather than a register deepening.
2. The image must appear only in the silence window — before the data review, not during or after. As soon as data appears, the image should be gone.
3. The image must never depict achievement, aspiration, or human presence. It must depict structure, not outcome.

## SECTION 7 — WHAT THE SCIENCE SAYS THE USER ACTUALLY WANTS

This is the section that matters most.

---

The user said: "I don't know — I want to focus on what the science says."

This is not indecision. It is a sophisticated signal from an Analytical/INTJ who has already noticed the aesthetic pull toward imagery, has already identified it as a potential distortion, and has chosen to seek an external reference before acting on it. This is the Analytical strength functioning correctly. The instinct to seek mechanism before making a visual decision is exactly the right instinct.

**Is the pull toward imagery a behavioral signal or an aesthetic preference?**

Both, and distinguishing them requires honesty. The pull toward imagery is:

1. **Partially aesthetic.** The mockups with imagery looked compelling. There is a legitimate human preference for visual richness that is not wrong to have. This is aesthetic preference — it is real, it matters to the user's subjective experience of the tool, and it should not be dismissed.

2. **Partially behavioral.** An app that feels more interesting to open is more likely to be opened. The premise that visual richness increases opening frequency is supported (Fogg, 2003, on perceived quality and engagement). This is not nothing.

3. **Partially a displacement activity.** This is the hardest part to say directly but the most important. Design sessions — working on the visual richness of a behavioral tool — are activities that feel productive and are not. For an Achiever who must produce daily to feel functional, a design session on imagery is production. It is also, at some level, a way to engage with the system's promise without engaging with the behaviors the system is designed to support. The Analytical profile building increasingly sophisticated systems rather than using them is the explicitly documented anti-pattern in INCREMENTS' own design guardrails. This analysis is part of that pattern.

The question is not "does imagery improve the app." The question is "what is the risk of spending more time on this question than on using the app."

**What does this profile actually need from the app visually?**

Not what they say they want. Not what would look good in a screenshot. What the science says.

An Analytical/INTJ in recovery, using a daily behavioral tool for 90+ days, needs from the visual environment:

**Precision.** Every element should be doing a specific job. The confidence that the system was designed with intent rather than dressed for appeal. This is the visual property that sustains trust over 90 days for this profile — not richness, not depth, not beauty. Precision.

**Evidence of intelligence.** Data that surfaces patterns the user didn't consciously track. Signals that arrive at the right moment. A scoring system that responds correctly to behavior. This is what makes a tool feel worth returning to — not imagery, but demonstrated understanding.

**Absence of manipulation.** This is the most important finding. The INTJ/Analytical profile detects "designed to be engaging" faster than any other profile type. Every element in the interface that is present for engagement rather than function is eventually identified and categorized as manipulative. Once that categorization occurs, it cannot be undone — the element erodes trust from that point forward. The safest long-term design decision is to never introduce elements that will eventually be categorized as manipulation.

**The honest answer:** The existing visual system, refined along the lines of the first audit, is already sufficient. The brain icon is the right amount of imagery. The operational register is the right atmospheric register. The data is the right depth source. The instinct to add imagery is an aesthetic preference, and satisfying it carries a non-zero risk — of consumption drift, of trust expenditure, of distraction from the behavioral question — that the aesthetic benefit does not offset.

This is not a verdict against imagery forever. If, at Day 90, the system has been used daily and the behavioral goals are substantially met, the user will be in a different state — less depleted, higher self-efficacy, more stable identity. At that point, the calculus changes. Imagery introduced after behavioral consolidation is decorative in a context that can afford decoration. Imagery introduced during recovery, during the critical 0–30 day window when the behavioral habit is forming, is a risk to the very thing the system is trying to build.

**The recommendation is not "no imagery." The recommendation is "not yet, and probably not much, and for specific reasons."**

Build the system. Use it. Let the data become the atmosphere. Then, from a position of established practice, evaluate whether the visual richness of the system needs to change.

That is what the science says.
